Derivatives and Polynomial Approximations
5/31/2017
The definition of the derivative that we learn in school is motivated geometrically through secant lines. While this is geometrically nice, it does little to emphasize the main purpose of derivatives: Approximation. We'll look at an equivalent way to define derivatives by trying to understand what we mean when we say that "The Tangent Line best approximates the function at a point" From this "best approximating line", we'll be able to recover the ordinary definition of the derivative. From there we can investigate higher order derivatives and see how Taylor polynomials have a natural interpretation in terms of approximation.
How to prove Fermat's Last Theorem
5/27/2017
Fermat's Last Theorem can be proved, once we have proved some other deep theorems. These theorems, however, can seem pretty unrelated to the statement of Fermat's Last Theorem. We'll discuss how to prove Fermat's Last Theorem assuming these deep results. We'll see how to prove Fermat's Last Theorem from the ABC-Conjecture, Serre's Conjecture and the Taniyama-Shimura Conjecture. This post will be a bit higher level than some others.
Why is Pi in the Gaussian Integral?
5/20/2017
The Gaussian Integral appears in various different contexts, from physics to probability to number theory. It can be surprising to find that its value relates to pi. It turns out that there is a much more concrete reason for why we can expect this integral to be related to pi, and it has to do with the properties of the function $f(x)=e^{x^2}$.

Complex Exponents and Euler's Formula.
5/01/2017
Euler's formula is a mysterious relationship between pi, e and i that can seem unintuitive. Many proofs are just proofs that it is true, rather than explorations on why it is true. Furthermore, most proofs rely on confusing visuals or properties of functions that are too abstract to be intuitive for the curious student that happens upon Euler's Formula. We'll give a "why" for Euler's formula and, more generally, see how trigonometry naturally pops up when you try to talk about raising numbers to complex powers.
Extending Exponents from "Repeated Multiplication" to Real Powers.
4/22/2017
Everyone knows that exponentiation is "repeated multiplication". But this only makes sense when the power is a positive integer. What do expressions like $A^{-4}$ or $A^{5/3}$ or $A^{\pi}$ mean? And why? In this post, we'll start with exponentiation as "repeated multiplication" and investigate the functional properties that make expontiation important in the first place, and then use this as the mechanism to allow us to make sense of powers that are not positive integers.
