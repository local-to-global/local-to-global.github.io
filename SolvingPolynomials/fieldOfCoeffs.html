Solving Polynomials 2: The Fields of Coefficients

11/21/2016

Let $F$ be a field, and let's say that we're trying to find an arbitrary solution to $ax^2+bx+c=0$. The idea is to work in the field $K=F(a,b,c)$ where we can find a field $L/K$ that splits the polynomial $ax^2+bx+c=0$ which will be a quadratic extension. If $F$ is not of degree two, then we can write $L=K(\sqrt{\Delta})$ for $\Delta=b^2-4ac$. This is all well-and-good, but given fixed $a,b,c\in F$, how do we use this to construct a field $E/F$ where $ax^2+bx+c$ splits? Moreover, how do the properties of the extension $L/K$ tell us about $E/F$?

We could just say that $E=F(\sqrt{\delta})$ where we have plugged in the appropriate values for $a,b,c$. And this does work. The issue is that this method does not offer a link between $L/K$ and $E/F$ through which we can transfer information. We'd have to independently justify that the extension is at most quadratic and that Galois information transfers in some way. This isn't too much of an issue in degree two, but we are losing an important tool in the general case and making more work for us. What we'd want is a map $K\rightarrow F$ and $L\rightarrow E$ through which the information is transferred in a way that we can analyze. The immediate problem with this is that there are no maps in this direction since $F$ is a subfield of $K$ and so the only additive and multiplicative map from $K$ to $F$ is the zero map, which does not provide any information. This shows that we need to work with alternate objects. Non-field rings perhaps.

Let's say that we fix $\alpha,\beta\gamma\in F$ and want to plug in $(a,b,c)=(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)$. This means immediately restricts the elements of $K=F(a,b,c)$ that we can work with. For instance, we can't work with $1/(a-\alpha)$. So any polynomial that we're going to be working with must not have at any of these coordinates. Let $R\subset K$ be the subring of elements $f(a,b,c)/g(a,b,c)$ in $K$ so that $g(\alpha,\beta,\gamma)\ne 0$. Note that $ax^2+bx+c\in R[x]$. With $R$, then, we have two maps involving $F$: The inclusion map $F\righarrow R$ and then the evaluation map $R\righarrow F$, the composition of these maps is the identity. Both $R$ and the evaluation map are dependent on the choice of $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$, and the kernel of the evaluation map will be a determinator of the information we're looking for. In particular, $R$ is a local ring and the $F$ is determined as the unique residue field at the maximal ideal of $R$.

We can construct $L$ in the same way, the splitting field of $ax^2+bx+c$, but we still want to be able to map to a corresponding field extension of $F$. To do this, let $S$ be the integral closure of $R$ in $L$. $S$ will be a semi-local field. The number of maximal ideals and their interactions determine different possibilities for field extensions of $F$ as we will discuss. But a key fact is that these are all isomorphic residue fields, each corresponding to how we choose roots. What we want from $S$ is a field $E$ which interacts with $S$ in the same way that $F$ interacts with $R$. That is, we want inclusion and evaluation maps $E\rightarrow S$ and $S\rightarrow E$ which extend the evaluations and inclusions of $R$ and $F$. So, morally, it should have all the information that we're looking for.
